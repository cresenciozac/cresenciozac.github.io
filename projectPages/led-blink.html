Astable Multivibrator, AKA... A LED Blinker.

April 7, 2026

Background

Back in March, I got super hyperfixated on the idea of making PCBs for some reason, and I was like: "Hey, you know what, I'm going to finally make my first one." So I got to work, and quickly realized that I had zero idea of what I was doing. I decided to make this small little "Astable Multivibrator" as they call it, but it is truly just a PCB that blinks two LEDs. It's like a "Hello World" of the electronic world, and I figured that I might as well complete that rite of passage first.

Below I'm going to quickly highlight the technical details and schematic, and then talk about my design process behind this lil' guy, and end it with some concluding thoughts.

Technical Details

To preface this, I had a very minimal knowledge of how to create my own PCB before this project. All I knew is that I had access to Altium through UMNSVP, and that I had to use my resources somehow. I looked up how to create a LED blinker on the internet and found a small schematic tutorial by DigiKey. Something that I find interesting about this circuit is that it's completely analog, doesn't use any microcontrollers or code to blink the LEDs, only using the properties of RC circuits and charged/discharged capacitors. The design consists of 2x475 Ω resistors, 2x47.5k Ω resistors, 2 LEDs, 2x10μF capacitors, and 2xNPN transistors. The PCB is powered through a 2-pin phoenix connector. The circuit works by the two transistors taking turns switching each other off, oscillating the LEDs back and forth. The larger the capacitance of C2 and C3, the slower the blink, as the timing is based upon the charging of these two capacitors. Also, a 100nF decoupling capacitor was placed next to the Phoenix connector to filter the noise from the power, keeping the oscillations clean.

Design Process

First, I created the circuit in Altium, as I mentioned earlier. I will say, although Altium was the standard for UMNSVP, KiCad is just so.. much.. better. Anyway, after creating the schematic, I layed out the PCB on Altium as well. I tried my best to keep everything as symmetrical as possible, and I put some fun silkscreen on the PCB as well. (Top right corner, my girlfriend and I's favorite lil' guy, Eggnog, :D)
Here's the same PCB in 3D.
After creating the PCB, I sent the files to OSH Park, a really nice PCB manufacturer based in the US (No tariffs!), and ordered my parts off Digikey. The PCB arrived a couple days later and here's a photo of the unsoldered PCB. In my opinion, the purple looks pretty sweet on it.
This was my first time soldering small electronics. I had experience soldering large Maxeon solar cells for UMNSVP, but I never had soldered components onto a PCB before, so it was a learning experience. I actually had to solder two of these, because my first one had a cold solder connection, causing a bug in the analog oscillations. The second time around, my solder connections were solid, and the oscillations worked just fine.

What I Learned

It was a fun little project. Mostly, I learned about soldering electrical components, component selection and shopping, creating PCBs on Altium, and how electrical components interact with each other. I also learned that PCBs are preetttyy expensive nowadays, so we just hope they go down in price soon.
